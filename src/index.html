Seoul

Seoul's allure lies in its captivating blend of tradition and modernity, offering travelers a dynamic urban experience steeped in rich history and vibrant culture. From the bustling streets of Myeongdong adorned with neon lights to the serene palaces of Gyeongbokgung, Seoul seamlessly intertwines ancient landmarks with contemporary marvels.

Top attractions

  1. Gyeongbokgung Palace

    As the largest of Seoul's Five Grand Palaces, Gyeongbokgung Palace stands as a majestic symbol of Korea's rich history and architectural grandeur. Visitors can stroll through its meticulously preserved halls, adorned with intricate artwork and surrounded by picturesque gardens, offering a glimpse into the royal past of the Joseon Dynasty.

    Link to Google Maps
  2. N Seoul Tower

    Perched atop Namsan Mountain, N Seoul Tower offers panoramic views of the sprawling cityscape below, making it a must-visit destination for breathtaking vistas and romantic sunsets. The tower's iconic structure, illuminated by a kaleidoscope of colors at night, serves as a beacon of Seoul's modernity and innovation.

    Link to Google Maps
  3. Bukchon Hanok Village

    Nestled amidst the bustling urban landscape, Bukchon Hanok Village preserves the charm of traditional Korean architecture with its labyrinthine alleyways and well-preserved hanok houses. Visitors can wander through its quaint streets, immersing themselves in the tranquil ambiance and timeless beauty of Korea's cultural heritage.

    Link to Google Maps
  4. Myeongdong Shopping Street

    Renowned as Seoul's premier shopping district, Myeongdong entices visitors with its eclectic mix of high-end boutiques, trendy fashion outlets, and bustling street markets. From skincare products to fashion accessories and delectable street food, Myeongdong offers a sensory feast for avid shoppers and food enthusiasts alike.

    Link to Google Maps
  5. Dongdaemum Design Plaza

    A beacon of innovation and creativity, the Dongdaemun Design Plaza (DDP) stands as a testament to Seoul's status as a global design hub. With its futuristic architecture and vibrant cultural events, including fashion shows and exhibitions, DDP showcases the city's unwavering commitment to artistic expression and cutting-edge design.

Cultural Differences

When traveling to Korea from a Western country, it's important to be mindful of certain cultural differences to ensure a respectful and enjoyable experience. Here are some key cultural differences to be aware of:

  • Hierarchy and Respect: Korean culture places a strong emphasis on hierarchy and respect for authority, age, and social status. It's customary to use formal language (honorifics) when speaking to elders or those in positions of authority. Bowing is also a common form of greeting and showing respect.
  • Personal Space: Koreans tend to have a smaller personal space bubble compared to Westerners. It's not uncommon for people to stand closer together while conversing or waiting in line. Be mindful of personal space boundaries and adjust your behavior accordingly.
  • Removing Shoes Indoors: In Korean homes, traditional restaurants, and even some businesses like spas and guesthouses, it's customary to remove your shoes before entering. Look for a designated area to leave your shoes or follow the lead of locals.
  • Dining Etiquette: When dining in Korea, there are certain etiquette rules to follow. Wait to be seated, use both hands when giving or receiving items (such as money or business cards), and never stick your chopsticks upright in a bowl of rice, as it resembles a funeral ritual.
  • Silence on Public Transportation: Unlike in some Western countries where conversations on public transportation are common, Koreans generally maintain a quiet atmosphere on buses and subways. Keep noise levels low and avoid speaking loudly or using your phone in crowded spaces.
